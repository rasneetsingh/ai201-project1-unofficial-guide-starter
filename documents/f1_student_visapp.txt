OVERVIEW (the three-step technique):
A college admissions counselor who works with international students lays out a framework for the F-1 visa interview: (1) Have a narrative, (2) Know your narrative, (3) Embrace your narrative.
 
STEP 1 — HAVE A NARRATIVE:
There is no single "good" answer for questions like "what will you do after graduation?" or "why this program?" What matters is that your narrative is (1) consistent with the law, (2) consistent with the rest of your narrative, and (3) consistent with common sense.
 
- Consistency with the law: The requirement isn't strictly to "return to your home country" — it's to demonstrate intent to *leave the US*. Where you go afterward doesn't matter, as long as you leave. Example: a student said he intended to do undergrad in the US and then a PhD in Germany (not return home), and the visa officer accepted it as sensible.
- Consistency with your narrative: Officers probe your past (what you studied, where you worked, how you chose schools) to see if you've behaved like someone with the goals you claim. Red flags: applying to very few schools, getting into everywhere you applied (not ambitious enough), or applying late and missing deadlines. You need a credible explanation for these. Core question: "Did you act like someone who has the future plans you claim?"
- Consistency with common sense ("the smell test"): Your career plan must make economic sense. Rule of thumb for graduate applicants: the salary "delta" (income with the degree minus income without it) should cover the total cost of education within about 5–6 years. Exceptions exist (hating your current job, upward mobility, wealthy family sponsorship), but you need *some* compelling justification. How funds are sourced matters too — loans invite more economic scrutiny; family sponsorship lowers the burden slightly but the path still has to make sense.
 
STEP 2 — KNOW YOUR NARRATIVE:
The US doesn't require an education plan or proof you can fund your *entire* degree (only the first year). The interview evaluates *you*, not just your credentials. The officer only knows what's on your I-20 and DS-160 — not where you applied, where you were accepted, your post-graduation plans, or exact funding sources, unless you tell them. So know your story cold, past and future: the schools you applied to, why, expected employment and salary, and possible career paths. Don't memorize scripted answers to question lists — know your details so you can answer anything. A useful test: do you know other students from your country who followed the same university → leave → career path? If no one has, it may signal the plan is infeasible, you're misrepresenting intent, or you haven't researched enough.
 
STEP 3 — EMBRACE YOUR NARRATIVE:
It's not enough to have a true story and know the details — you must be convinced of its feasibility, or you won't convince the officer. Your eligibility depends on *present intent*, not hopes and dreams. Analogy: wanting to drive a Ferrari but buying a Toyota — wishing you could stay in the US isn't illegal as long as your present intent is to leave. If asked "would you like to live in the US permanently?", you don't have to say "no"; a response like "it might be nice, but my present intent is to leave at the conclusion of my studies" can be legitimate. Walk up with confidence — appearing nervous about red flags can itself become a red flag.
 
Q&A FROM COMMENTS (real applicant situations + advice):
 
- School reputation: Don't worry that a school is known mainly for STEM (e.g., Georgia Tech for a non-STEM master's). If your research shows the path is viable, be confident; find the closest parallel you can.
- Non-immediate-family sponsors: A sponsor who isn't a parent (e.g., uncle, dad's cousin) draws more suspicion than a parent, because non-immediate family is more likely to change priorities, and repeated family sponsorships can suggest a pattern of migration. Still, a non-parent sponsor is better than no sponsor. Whether you'll be asked why parents aren't funding depends on your situation/country.
- Wanting to work in the US after a PhD: You're allowed to be open to working in the US, but don't say it's the *only* place you're considering. A fully funded PhD from a reputable university makes approval likelihood fairly high.
- Community college → transfer to a 4-year: It's fine to disclose plans to transfer to a bachelor's program afterward. The requirement is that you intend to leave the US at the conclusion of your *studies*, not at the end of a particular program. Students who disclosed transfer plans have been approved.
- Staying near US-based family: It's not illegal or inherently suspicious to want to live near a sibling. But officers look for a pattern of migration, so have a clear narrative that you intend to leave.
- "Why this school?" / "Why this major?": Weak answers focus only on facilities, helping people, or enjoying biology — none point to a goal *outside the US*. Better answers tie the program to a concrete post-graduation goal back home or abroad.
- Narrative credibility check: If you claim you'll (e.g.) study nursing in the US then work for an NGO in your home country, but you don't know anyone who has actually done that as a salaried path, the officer is likely to be skeptical.
- PhD → professor in home country: A credible narrative if such positions exist there; be ready to name several universities where you'd want to work. You don't have to be fixated on returning to exactly your home country.
- Second-attempt after rejection: One thrice-rejected applicant from a low-approval country succeeded by telling the officer his circumstances hadn't changed and asking for a chance to explain his living situation.
- Example of an unsuccessful interview (self-reported): An MBA applicant felt he overshared about the family farm/lime trees instead of giving a professional reason for the MBA, and fumbled a "what are you doing now?" question by initially saying "taking care of my grandma" before mentioning a J-1 internship.
